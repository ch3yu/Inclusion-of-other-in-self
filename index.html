Continuous IOS — Repeated Measurement

A participant reports interpersonal closeness across 20 trials. Each response is recorded and emailed to the researcher on completion.

About this measure The core idea of inclusion of other in self (IOS), developed primarily by psychologist Arthur Aron and his colleagues in the early 1990s, is that we begin to incorporate aspects of someone into our own sense of who we are when we form a close relationship with that person — the boundary between “me” and “you” becomes porous, and their resources, perspectives, and identity start to feel, on some level, like our own. IOS was developed to measure interpersonal closeness, and it was validated against established, longer, and more complicated closeness questionnaires when Aron and colleagues introduced the scale — and it held up impressively well.

Before you begin

On each screen you'll see two circles — one is you, the other is the target you name.

Type the target's name, then drag the slider to set how much the circles overlap. The overlap of the circles represents the connection you feel towards the other participant. For example, a stronger connection between you and the other participant would be represented by a greater overlap of the circles. Find the degree of overlap that best represents the connection you feel towards the other participant during the trial.

You'll do this 20 times. There are no right or wrong answers — respond honestly each time.
